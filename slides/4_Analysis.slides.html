Data Management and Organization

Data organization

  • Many of us waste a lot of time...
  • Hunting down data files
  • Trying to figure out what exactly we did
  • Hoping nothing bad happens to our data

    • Any convention is better than no convention

  • There are better and worse ways to organize data
  • But pretty much anything is better than saving to random locations on your drive
  • Come up with some convention and stick with it. E.g...
  • StudyName/
  • Data/
  • Scripts/
  • Analyses/
  • Results/
  • And similarly for tools you might apply to different datasets
  • There are packages designed expressly for this purpose
  • E.g., John Myles White's ProjectTemplate package for R

    • Managing and manipulating data

  • Data are rarely in the right form the first time around
  • R, Matlab, Python, etc. all feature extensive tools for data organization
  • Use split-apply-combine liberally

    • Relational databases

  • Data are organized as a series of interlinked tables
  • Useful when you need to store/access large amounts of data
  • Why?
  • Eliminates redundancy
  • Faster access to data
  • Allows complex structured queries
  • Most DBs are SQL-based
  • Provides a flexible and fast way to access information on-the-fly

    • Predictive modeling

    What's the point of analyzing our data?

  • To test a hypothesis
  • To quantify the size of an effect
  • To identify novel associations
  • To predict an outcome
  • In descending order of (perceived) importance in psychology
  • Debatable, of course!

    • Hypothesis testing

  • Arguably the paradigmatic way to do psychological science

  • Come up with a hypothesis, design an experiment to test it

  • In theory (and often practice), this works phenomenally well

  • But, some pitfalls:
  • The most common statistical approaches make little sense (Krantz, 1999)
  • p values are widely misunderstood and misused
  • The null is always false
  • Hypothesis testing often collapses into hypothesis confirmation
  • "False-positive" psychology (Simmons, Nelson, & Simonsohn, 2011)
    • "If the data did not confirm the hypothesis, I talked to people in the lab about what needs to be done next, which would typically involve brainstorming about what needs to be changed, implementing the changes, preparing the new study and re-running it." --Jens Foerster

    Effect size estimation

  • Not just "is there an effect?", but "how big is the effect?"
  • This is probably what we actually care about--not just knowing the sign
  • More sensible statistical methods
  • E.g., confidence intervals in place (or addition to) p values
  • But, some pitfalls:
  • Estimation requires more data
  • People are less excited about your results
  • Easy to overfit and overstate one's findings (Ioannidis, 2008)

    • Exploration

  • A noble (if underappreciated goal)
  • The world is complicated and we aren't that smart
  • How many interesting but non-intuitive findings are out there?
  • Some pitfalls:
  • How do we avoid "fishing" expeditions?
  • The bigger the data, the more potential comparisons are available
  • Strong pressure to present exploratory results as confirmatory ones

    • Prediction

  • Outside of applied areas, not taken very seriously
  • "Our goal is to understand the mind, not to predict behavior!" --Many Psychologists
  • Ability to predict certainly isn't sufficient for understanding
  • But it's almost certainly necessary

    • Don't we already get prediction for free?

  • Isn't effect size a measure of how well we could predict something?
  • Many psychology articles report predicting outcomes with high accuracy
  • Isn't that just what R^2 is?
  • No!
  • Psychologists almost never actually predict anything
  • Why not?

    • Overfitting

  • The "cardinal sin" in machine learning
  • Most commonly due to the use of same data for training and testing
  • Classical estimation techniques fit both signal and noise
  • Compounded by researcher degrees of freedom
  • E.g., which of 5 models should you report? The one that works best!
  • But avoiding "p-hacking" still doesn't mean your model is performing well
  • The more flexible your model, the better your results will seem
  • But generalization will suffer

    # Example borrowed in whole from scikit-learn docs
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline
from sklearn.preprocessing import PolynomialFeatures
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression


np.random.seed(0)

n_samples = 30
degrees = [1, 4, 15]

true_fun = lambda X: np.cos(1.5 * np.pi * X)
X = np.sort(np.random.rand(n_samples))
y = true_fun(X) + np.random.randn(n_samples) * 0.1

plt.figure(figsize=(14, 4))
for i in range(len(degrees)):
    ax = plt.subplot(1, len(degrees), i+1)
    plt.setp(ax, xticks=(), yticks=())

    polynomial_features = PolynomialFeatures(degree=degrees[i],
                                             include_bias=False)
    linear_regression = LinearRegression()
    pipeline = Pipeline([("polynomial_features", polynomial_features),
                         ("linear_regression", linear_regression)])
    pipeline.fit(X[:, np.newaxis], y)

    X_test = np.linspace(0, 1, 100)
    plt.plot(X_test, pipeline.predict(X_test[:, np.newaxis]), label="Model")
    plt.plot(X_test, true_fun(X_test), label="True function")
    plt.scatter(X, y, label="Samples")
    plt.xlabel("x")
    plt.ylabel("y")
    plt.xlim((0, 1))
    plt.ylim((-2, 2))
    plt.legend(loc="best")
    plt.title("Degree %d" % degrees[i])
plt.show()

  • Let's simulate overfitting as a function of:

    • Machine learning

    A computer program is said to learn from experience E with respect to some class of tasks T and performance measure P, if its performance at tasks in T, as measured by P, improves with experience E (Mitchell, 1997)
  • Or, more simply: a field concerned with developing predictive algorithms that improve performance as the amount of available data increases

  • Covers an enormous range of approaches
  • Classical regression, Bayesian modeling, clustering algorithms, penalized regression, support vector machines, decision trees, etc.
  • More of a way of thinking about problems than a distinct field of statistics or CS
  • Virtually any statistical model can be approached from a ML perspective
  • How is this relevant to psychology?

    • Prediction is hard: the Netflix Prize

  • $1 million dollars to the first group to improve rating predictions by 10%
  • Took 3 years, tens of thousands of person hours to win
  • Diminishing returns set in quickly
  • ...and the solution was never implemented:
    • "We evaluated some of the new methods offline but the additional accuracy gains that we measured did not seem to justify the engineering effort needed to bring them into a production environment."
  • Real-world prediction is hard
  • Much harder than you would think from reading psychology articles

    • Why so difficult?

  • Three different problems
  • Feature extraction/engineering
  • Feature selection: Deciding which features to use
  • Predictive modeling: Combining the chosen features optimally
  • Might be easier to see in a genetic context
  • What kinds of features do we use? SNPs? CNVs? Gene expression levels? Etc...
  • Given 1,000,000 SNPs, which ones should we focus on?
  • Suppose you identify 500 'good' SNPs, how should we combine them to generate a prediction?
  • There's no free lunch (seriously)

    • Feature engineering and feature selection

  • Familiar problems to most psychologists
  • Much/most of psychology can be recast in these terms
  • Which variables matter for predicting the outcome you care about?
  • Almost completely open-ended
  • You can always think of more things to measure that could make a difference
  • What "predicts"...
  • Where people like to live?
  • Who dates who?
  • Which candidate you vote for?
  • How quickly you respond to a stimulus on the 32nd trial of a color-word Stroop task?

    • The feature selection problem in dating

  • What features would help you predict whether two people are compatible?
  • Physical appearance
  • Personality traits
  • Interests and vocations
  • Political and religious attitudes
  • Desire for a family
  • Expectations
  • Cultural norms
  • Circadian rhythms
  • Fashion sense
  • That annoying thing he does when he taps his teeth with his fingers, urghhhh, I can't stand it
  • Etc. etc. etc. etc. etc. etc.
  • Nooooooo! So many features!
  • All of these may have "large" effects, yet still predict very little. Why?
  • Worse, the important features will vary by person!
  • Worse still, there are bound to be all kinds of interactions!!

    • Which features?

  • Anything you can measure that might predict the outcome is a feature
  • There's no obvious limit to the number of potential features
  • We can't model all of them, so we need to select the right ones
  • How do we know which ones are the right ones?
  • Domain expertise
  • Cleverness
  • Experimentation
  • Algorithmic approaches
  • With bad features, no amount of clever analysis will help you
  • With good features, even simple predictive models may do quite well
  • Recasting psychological questions in these terms can be helpful
  • The world is complex, most phenomena will have many contributing factors

    • Which model?

  • You've come up with a bunch of features! Congratulations!
  • Now it's time to use that knowledge
  • How do we generate accurate predictions?
  • Generic Psychologist's response: USE MULTIPLE REGRESSION!
  • But this isn't a good approach when you have a lot of features
  • Why?

    The bias-variance tradeoff

  • The more you trust the data in front of you, the less well you can generalize
  • Need to capture the regularities without capturing the noise
  • High bias leads to underfitting; high variance leads to overfitting
  • Ordinary least-squares regression is a low-bias, high-variance method

    • The bias-variance tradeoff

    http://scott.fortmann-roe.com/docs/BiasVariance.html

    How can we assess generalization performance?

  • Best way is true generalization--test a new sample!
  • But that's hard/expensive
  • A simple approach that's still pretty good:

    cross-validation

  • Use different subsets of the data to train and test
  • Produces minimally biased estimates of model fit
  • Most common strategy: k-folds cross-validation
  • Cross-validation can be used with any model, including multiple regression
  • So you have no excuse for reporting crazy R^2 values!

    • K-fold Cross-validation

    http://chrisjmccormick.wordpress.com/2013/07/31/k-fold-cross-validation-with-matlab-code/

    Supervised vs. unsupervised learning

  • Supervised learning: develop a model that can classify or predict cases whose true value is already know
  • E.g., developing an algorithm to predict whether or not someone meets criteria for depression, where clinician diagnosis is the gold standard
  • Ideal when the ground truth is known for a training sample and the goal is to generalize to new data
  • Unsupervised learning: identify clusters or groups of observations without any prior knowledge of their membership
  • E.g., identify clusters of individuals who show similar patterns of depressive symptoms
  • Unconstrained by prior expectations, and may have greater potential to reveal new knowledge

    • Clustering methods

    http://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/clustering.html

    Putting it together

  • In practice, feature selection and predictive modeling are usually done interactively and iteratively:
  • Select some features
  • Fit some models
  • Evaluate the results
  • Repeat and refine
  • This is a sensible--perhaps necessary--approach
  • But it can still be dangerous. Why?
  • If you keep tweaking the model based on the 'cross-validated' results, they're no longer truly unbiased
  • There's no free lunch!

    • Why should we care?

  • An effect is rarely interesting unless it generalizes out-of-sample
  • Our models must be able to approximate the complexity of the thing we're studying
  • Machine learning techniques aren't necessarily blackboxes
  • They're powerful tools that can be used to advance theory
